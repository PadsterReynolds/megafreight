Customs VAT Regulations 2 years ago

A number of changes continue to be made to VAT procedures, some relating to more than just proof of payment of VAT, such as ownership of the goods and the timing of invoicing of Customs VAT along with impending changes to the Customs Act all of which should be carefully noted.

The latest legislative VAT timing rules affect our South African import clients as it further limits the period when customs VAT falls due and when it may be claimed, summarised as follows;

  1. VAT is payable on the importation of tangible goods into South Africa by the importer. The importer may claim the import VAT as a deduction if he is registered for VAT and acquires the goods for making taxable supplies, but SARS requires that the importer must be the owner of the imported goods. SARSâ€™ view is that the import VAT does not qualify as a deduction if the importer does not own the goods even if they are used to make taxable supplies.
  2. From 1 April 2014, import VAT can only be claimed during the same tax period when the goods were imported and the same tax period VAT is paid to Customs by us (this regulation has been postponed several times since this date).
  3. In the same vein the Customs Act 91 of 1964 requires goods to be cleared seven (7) days prior to arrival of the vessel (aircraft, ship or vehicle) but the new draft act states that goods must be cleared three (3) days prior (failing which SARS will impose a penalties).

What we see as important about this is that relaxation and extension of these regulations by SA Revenue Services (SARS) will soon lapse and importers can expect increased enforcement in the near future. The new Customs Act is expected to come into force early next year.

What are we doing? Insofar as the requirement that import VAT can only be claimed by you during the same tax period when the goods were imported our system has been upgraded to ensure as far as possible that the VAT is invoiced during the same month that the goods are imported.

This does however sometimes present a problem when we are forced to clear goods for example on the last day of the month to avoid penalties. We may not be ready to invoice the shipment on the same day because we don't have all the charges or the shipment has been detained for Customs examination. We are trying to seek clarification but in the meantime invoicing will be delayed in such cases.

Regarding proof of payment we are presently busy with changes to reflect the eFiling payment reference against individual VAT records that will be mailed to you, not an altogether easy task to complete because most accounting systems are not able to recognise customs VAT let alone which client it belongs to (SARS remain unreasonably visionless and unsympathetic to these problems). This means that in cases where the import VAT happens to be paid to Customs by us in the next tax period via our deferment scheme, you can only claim the import VAT during that tax period.

What must you do? The procedure was recently changed where the agent is no longer required to provide the statement contemplated in section 54(3)(b)(ii) provided customs documentation is held by the importer. You will therefore be entitled to an input tax deduction in terms of section 16(3) read with section 16(2)(d), provided that at the time any return in respect of the importation is furnished, you hold the following;

  1. Customs documentation; and
  2. Proof of payment of the VAT by your agent.

Please note SARS requires that the importer must be the owner of the imported goods and, while we believe this has always been the case, it seems that SARS will pay far closer attention to the ownership side of things in future. Importers would be well advised to remain acutely aware of this particularly when requesting us to invoice another separately registered vendor VAT inclusive (sometimes within the same group) with the intention for that vendor to claim the customs VAT.

And finally, clients who withhold payment of our invoices because goods were only delivered in the month following our invoice date are asked to kindly take into consideration the following;

  1. It is obviously impossible for us to take delivery of imported goods prior to clearance of the goods;
  2. We are compelled to invoice customs VAT during the same tax period when the goods were imported in order for you to be entitled to claim the VAT;
  3. Additional regulations state that tax invoices must be raised within 21 days of performing the service; and
  4. It is not always possible to deliver goods in the same month that we are compelled to raise the invoice containing Customs VAT.

Although payments withheld for this reason consists of a small percentage it nonetheless places additional stress on our cash flow and we appeal to these clients to please ensure our invoices are paid by the due date indicated therein failing which interest will be raised at 2% per month (or part thereof).
